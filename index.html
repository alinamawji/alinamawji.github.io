Breast Cancer Incidence and Survival Factors

Welcome to the interactive slideshow on breast cancer incidence and survival factors. This presentation will cover various factors that play a role in breast cancer incidence and survival, including age, cancer stage, tumor size, race, and marital status.


Important facts:

  • Breast cancer is the most common cancer among women worldwide
  • Certain factors can increase risk, such as age, family history, and inherited gene mutations
  • Detecting breast cancer early through regular screenings significantly improves survival rates

For more details, check out the dataset on Kaggle.

What is the age distribution of breast cancer cases?

You may wonder around what age breast cancer develops. According to the American Cancer Society, the median age of breast cancer diagnosis is age 62. This means half of the women who developed breast cancer are 62 years old or younger. This slide shows a graphical representation of breast cancer cases by age group, and segmented by cancer stage.


Key takeaways:

  • Breast cancer incidence is most common in women ages 40 and older
  • Stage 1 and 2 cancers are the most common

Does tumor size play a role in estimated survival time?

One measure that is tracked during breast cancer treatment is tumor size. Tumor size is a predictor for survival of various cancers, but this is not always the case. This slide shows a graphical representation of tumor size by estimated survival months for breast cancer. It is further segmented by cancer stage.


Key takeaways:

  • Tumor size does not correlate with the estimated months of survival
  • Generally, as cancer stages progresses (from T1 to T4), tumor size increases
  • Estimated months of survival can be due to other factors

Do demographic factors show differences in survival?

Once you are diagnosed with breast cancer, you may wonder whether any demographic factors correlate with your survival. Some studies have indicated race and marital status to be predictors of breast cancer survival. This slide shows a graphical representation of either race or marital status by percentage of survival vs death.


Key takeaways:

  • Other races have higher rates of survival compared to white and black women
  • Married women have higher rates of survival compared to other marital statuses
